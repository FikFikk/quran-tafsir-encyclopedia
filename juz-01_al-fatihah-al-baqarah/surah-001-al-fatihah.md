# Surah Al-Fatihah (The Opening)

**Surah Number**: 1  
**Revelation**: Makki  
**Verses**: 7  
**Position in Quran**: The Opening Chapter  
**Other Names**: Umm al-Kitab (Mother of the Book), As-Sab' al-Mathani (The Seven Oft-Repeated), Al-Hamd (The Praise), Ash-Shifa (The Healing), As-Salah (The Prayer)

---

## Tafsir (Commentary)

### Overview
Al-Fatihah is the most recited chapter of the Quran, repeated at least 17 times daily in the five obligatory prayers. Despite its brevity, it encapsulates the core message of the entire Quran: the recognition of Allah's lordship, the pursuit of guidance, and the human need for divine assistance.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: "Al-Fatihah is the greatest surah in the Quran" (Bukhari). In another hadith, Allah says in a hadith qudsi: "I have divided the prayer (Al-Fatihah) between Myself and My servant into two halves" (Muslim).

### Structure
The surah is divided into two parts:
- **Verses 1-4**: Praise and acknowledgment of Allah (haqq Allah)
- **Verses 5-7**: The servant's requests and needs (haqq al-'abd)

### Verse-by-Verse Tafsir

#### Verse 1: Bismillahi ar-Rahmani ar-Rahim

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: The Basmalah is a blessing and seeking of divine assistance. Every significant action should begin with Allah's name. "Ar-Rahman" emphasizes Allah's general mercy to all creation, while "Ar-Rahim" emphasizes His specific mercy to believers.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: Beginning with Allah's name is a Sunnah established by the Prophet ﷺ and found at the opening of his letters. It teaches humility—we acknowledge that all success comes from Allah, not ourselves.

**Modern Insight (Quraish Shihab)**: The Basmalah conditions the believer to remember Allah in all actions, transforming mundane activities into worship when done with proper intention. "Ar-Rahman" relates to Allah's mercy in this world (provision, health), while "Ar-Rahim" relates to the afterlife (forgiveness, Paradise).

**Linguistic Note**: 
- **Ism** (name) represents the essence and attributes of the named
- **Allah**: The proper name of the One True God, not derived from any other word
- **Ar-Rahman**: From rahma (mercy), on the pattern fa'lan indicating intensity and permanence
- **Ar-Rahim**: On the pattern fa'il indicating continuous action

#### Verse 2: Alhamdu lillahi Rabbi al-'alamin

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: "Alhamd" (praise) is more comprehensive than "shukr" (thanks). Shukr is for favor received, while hamd is for Allah's inherent perfection whether we receive favor or not. "Rabb" means Lord, Master, Sustainer, and Nurturer. "Al-'alamin" (the worlds) encompasses all creation: humans, jinn, angels, animals, plants, and all that exists in the seen and unseen realms.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: This verse establishes that Allah alone deserves praise, cutting off any inclination to praise creation independently of the Creator. When we praise someone's skills or character, we are ultimately praising Allah who granted those qualities.

**Tafsir Al-Tabari**: The definite article "al" in "alhamdu" indicates that all forms of praise, in all times and places, belong to Allah. Not some praise, but all praise.

**Sayyid Qutb**: This verse cultivates a worldview where the believer sees all blessings as coming from Allah, leading to perpetual gratitude and contentment. The believer is never ungrateful because they trace every good back to its divine source.

**Practical Application**: 
- Begin with gratitude before making requests
- Recognize Allah's lordship in all circumstances, not just when convenient
- See all of creation as Allah's domain, submitted to His will

#### Verse 3: Ar-Rahmani ar-Rahim

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: The repetition of these two attributes after the Basmalah emphasizes Allah's mercy as the dominant characteristic of His interaction with creation. Though Allah is also Al-Jabbar (The Compeller) and Al-Muntaqim (The Avenger), His mercy precedes and encompasses His other attributes.

**Hadith Connection**: The Prophet ﷺ said, "When Allah created creation, He wrote in His Book, which is with Him upon the Throne: 'My Mercy overcomes My Wrath'" (Bukhari & Muslim).

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: These attributes are mentioned three times in the first four verses (once in Basmalah, once here), emphasizing that approaching Allah should be with hope in His mercy, not despair due to sins.

**Modern Reflection**: In a world filled with hardship, these repeated references to mercy provide psychological and spiritual comfort. Allah's default mode is mercy, not punishment.

#### Verse 4: Maliki Yawmi ad-Din

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: "Malik" means absolute owner and sovereign. "Yawm ad-Din" is the Day of Judgment when all will be recompensed. On that Day, all earthly claims to ownership and power dissolve—only Allah's authority remains.

**Variant Reading**: Some qira'at (readings) use "Maaliki" (King) instead of "Maliki" (Owner). Both are authentic. "Maalik" emphasizes sovereignty and dominion, while "Malik" emphasizes ownership and possession. Ibn Kathir notes both meanings are true—Allah is both King and Owner.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: This verse connects mercy (verses 1-3) with accountability (verse 4). Allah's mercy does not negate justice. The one who hopes in Allah's mercy must also prepare for standing before Him in judgment.

**Wisdom of Placement**: After establishing Allah's mercy, the Surah mentions the Day of Judgment to balance hope with fear—the two wings upon which the believer flies, as the early scholars said.

**Practical Application**:
- Live with awareness that all actions are recorded and will be judged
- Balance hope in Allah's mercy with mindfulness of accountability
- Recognize that worldly status means nothing on the Day of Judgment

#### Verse 5: Iyyaka na'budu wa iyyaka nasta'in

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: This is the pivot of the Surah. The pronoun "iyyaka" (You alone) is placed before the verbs for emphasis—restricting worship and seeking help exclusively to Allah. This embodies Tawhid (Islamic monotheism) in both belief and action.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: "Na'budu" (we worship) encompasses all forms of obedience, submission, and devotion. "Nasta'in" (we seek help) includes reliance on Allah for both religious and worldly matters. The sequence is significant: worship comes before asking for help, teaching that our relationship with Allah is not merely transactional.

**Hadith Qudsi**: Allah says, "This is between Me and My servant, and My servant shall have what he has asked for." When the servant says "You alone we worship," Allah says, "This is for Me." When he says "You alone we ask for help," Allah says, "This is for My servant, and My servant shall have what he has asked for" (Muslim).

**Grammatical Significance**: The plural "we" instead of "I" indicates:
1. Humility (royal plural showing respect)
2. Unity with other believers in worship
3. The communal nature of Islamic practice

**Sayyid Qutb**: This verse defines the Muslim's position: complete servitude to Allah alone liberates humans from servitude to other humans, ideologies, desires, and material pursuits. True freedom is found in worshiping the Creator alone.

**Practical Application**:
- Examine all actions to ensure they are for Allah's sake alone (ikhlas)
- Seek Allah's help before beginning any task
- Recognize dependence on Allah in all states: health, wealth, knowledge, success

#### Verse 6: Ihdina as-sirata al-mustaqim

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: "Ihdina" can mean "guide us to the path" (for those who haven't found it) or "keep us on the path" (for those already on it). Both meanings are intended. "As-sirat" is the path, and "al-mustaqim" means straight—the shortest, most direct route to Allah without deviation.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: The straight path is Islam, with its beliefs, laws, and ethics. It is "straight" because it is balanced—neither excessive nor deficient in any aspect. It avoids the extremes of other religious traditions.

**Hadith Connection**: The Prophet ﷺ drew a straight line and said, "This is Allah's path." Then he drew lines branching off on both sides and said, "These are other paths, and at the head of each path is a devil calling people to it." Then he recited, "And indeed, this is My straight path, so follow it, and do not follow other ways" (Quran 6:153) (Ahmad, authenticated by Al-Albani).

**Al-Ghazali**: The straight path has three dimensions:
1. **Belief**: Correct understanding of Allah, prophethood, and the Hereafter
2. **Character**: Purification of the heart from spiritual diseases
3. **Action**: Adherence to Shariah in worship and dealings

**Why Ask Repeatedly?**: We ask for guidance in every rak'ah of every prayer because:
1. Guidance is not static—it's a continuous need as new situations arise
2. The heart is constantly vulnerable to deviation
3. There are levels of guidance—we always need deeper understanding and firmer commitment

**Practical Application**:
- Make du'a sincerely for guidance, not as rote recitation
- Study Islam to know what the straight path entails
- Examine life regularly to identify deviations and correct them

#### Verse 7: Sirata alladhina an'amta 'alayhim...

**Tafsir Ibn Kathir**: This verse clarifies whose path we seek. "Those whom You have favored" are explained in Surah An-Nisa (4:69): "The prophets, the truthful, the martyrs, and the righteous." 

The "those who earned anger" are primarily those who know the truth but reject or distort it, such as the Jews who were given knowledge but failed to act upon it. The "those who are astray" are those who are ignorant of the truth, such as the Christians who deviated into worshiping Jesus despite their sincerity.

**Tafsir Al-Qurtubi**: The verse teaches that faith requires three elements:
1. Knowledge of truth (lacking in the misguided)
2. Acting upon knowledge (lacking in those who earned anger)
3. Divine favor and assistance (which we continuously request)

**Hadith Connection**: When the Prophet ﷺ recited this verse, the angel Jibril said, "Say: Ameen," and the Prophet ﷺ said "Ameen" (Abu Dawud). The Prophet ﷺ taught, "When the imam says 'wa la ad-dallin,' say 'Ameen,' for the angels say 'Ameen,' and whoever's Ameen coincides with the angels' Ameen will have all their previous sins forgiven" (Bukhari & Muslim).

**Sayyid Qutb**: This verse shows that sincerity alone is not enough—one must also have correct knowledge. Conversely, knowledge alone is insufficient without sincere implementation. Both are needed, and both require Allah's favor.

**Contemporary Relevance**: 
- Beware of two modern pitfalls: blind religiosity without knowledge (extremism), and knowledge without spiritual connection (secularism)
- Recognize that Muslims are not immune from falling into the errors of past nations
- Seek beneficial knowledge and righteous action together

**Practical Application**:
- Study the lives of prophets, companions, and righteous scholars as practical models
- Avoid arrogance in knowledge—the Jews had knowledge but earned Allah's anger
- Avoid ignorance masked as spirituality—the Christians were sincere but misguided
- Say "Ameen" with presence of heart after reciting Al-Fatihah

## Asbabun Nuzul (Circumstances of Revelation)

Al-Fatihah was revealed in Makkah during the early period of prophethood. There is scholarly consensus that it was among the first complete surahs revealed. Some scholars state it was the first surah revealed in its entirety, while others hold that distinction for Surah Al-Alaq or Al-Muddaththir (which were revealed in portions).

**Ibn Abbas** reported that Al-Fatihah was revealed in Makkah (Tafsir Ibn Kathir).

**Revelation Context**: 
- The Muslim community was small and persecuted
- The prayer structure was being established
- The surah was given as the foundational prayer text—to be recited in every rak'ah

**Wisdom**: That Al-Fatihah came early shows that prayer and direct connection with Allah are the foundation of faith, preceding most legal rulings and detailed commandments.

## Key Themes and Lessons

### 1. Tawhid (Oneness of Allah)
- Exclusive worship of Allah (verse 5)
- All praise belongs to Allah (verse 2)
- Lordship over all worlds (verse 2)
- Sovereignty over the Day of Judgment (verse 4)

### 2. Balance of Hope and Fear
- Hope: Emphasized through repeated mention of mercy (verses 1, 3)
- Fear: Established through mention of judgment and accountability (verse 4)

### 3. Human Dependence on Divine Guidance
- We cannot guide ourselves (verse 6)
- Guidance is an ongoing need, not a one-time achievement
- We need Allah's help even to worship Him properly (verse 5)

### 4. The Middle Path
- The straight path is between extremes
- Avoiding the error of knowledge without action (those who earned anger)
- Avoiding the error of devotion without knowledge (those who went astray)

### 5. Servitude as Liberation
- Worshiping Allah alone frees us from servitude to others
- Seeking Allah's help liberates us from dependence on creation
- True dignity comes from being Allah's servant

## Virtues and Benefits of Al-Fatihah

### From Hadith

1. **The Greatest Surah**: "By Him in Whose Hand is my soul, nothing like it has been revealed in the Torah, the Gospel, the Zabur, or even the rest of the Quran" (Bukhari)

2. **Healing**: "Fatihatul Kitab (Al-Fatihah) is a cure for every illness" (Darimi, authenticated by Al-Albani). A companion used it to recite over a person bitten by a scorpion, and he was healed (Bukhari).

3. **Light**: "Allah has not revealed the like of Umm al-Quran (Al-Fatihah) in the Torah or the Injeel. It is the seven oft-repeated verses and the great Quran that I have been given" (Bukhari)

4. **No Prayer Without It**: "No prayer is valid for the one who does not recite Fatihatul Kitab" (Bukhari & Muslim)

5. **Split Between Allah and Servant**: In the hadith qudsi mentioned earlier, Allah personally responds to each verse as it is recited (Muslim)

### Spiritual Benefits (from Scholars' Experience)

- **Presence of Heart**: Forces focus and concentration 17+ times daily
- **Remembrance**: Keeps purpose of life at the forefront of consciousness
- **Humility**: Repeated acknowledgment of neediness and dependence
- **Gratitude**: Begins with praise, cultivating thankfulness
- **Hope**: Mercy mentioned before judgment

## Practical Applications

### In Daily Prayer
1. Recite with understanding, not mechanically
2. Pause after each verse to reflect on its meaning
3. Personalize the du'a: truly ask for guidance
4. Say "Ameen" with certainty that Allah will respond

### In Daily Life
1. Begin tasks with Bismillah, seeking Allah's blessing
2. Maintain balance between hope (mercy) and fear (judgment)
3. Examine your path regularly—are you on the straight path?
4. Study the lives of those whom Allah favored (prophets, companions, righteous scholars)
5. Be vigilant against the two major deviations: knowledge without action, action without knowledge

### For Spiritual Development
1. **Morning & Evening**: Recite with full presence, reflecting on each verse
2. **When Seeking Guidance**: Recite repeatedly with sincere du'a for clarity in decisions
3. **When Ill or Distressed**: Use as ruqyah (spiritual healing), as the companions did
4. **When Teaching Children**: Explain each verse so they understand what they're saying to Allah

## Scholarly Differences (Ikhtilaf)

### 1. Is Basmalah Part of Al-Fatihah?

**Opinion 1 (Shafi'i, Ahmad in one narration)**: The Basmalah is verse 1 of Al-Fatihah and must be recited aloud in prayer when Al-Fatihah is recited aloud.

**Opinion 2 (Hanafi, Maliki, Hanbali mainstream)**: The Basmalah is a separate verse revealed as a marker between surahs. It should be recited silently before Al-Fatihah in prayer.

**Evidence for Opinion 1**: Hadith where the Prophet ﷺ said, "The prayer is divided between Me and My servant: half of it for Me and half for him. When My servant says 'Alhamdu lillahi Rabb al-'alamin'..." (Muslim). The hadith continues through all seven verses, implying Basmalah is included in the count.

**Evidence for Opinion 2**: Various narrations indicate the Prophet ﷺ used to recite the opening takbir, then pause briefly, then recite Al-Fatihah silently beginning with Basmalah (Abu Dawud).

**Reconciliation**: Both practices are valid based on sound evidence. The difference does not affect the validity of prayer.

### 2. Malik or Maalik (Verse 4)?

**Qira'ah 1**: مَالِكِ (Maliki) - Owner/Possessor of the Day of Judgment

**Qira'ah 2**: مَلِكِ (Maaliki) - King/Sovereign of the Day of Judgment

**Status**: Both are authentic, mutawatir recitations. Scholars say both convey true attributes of Allah, complementing rather than contradicting each other.

### 3. Saying Ameen Aloud or Silently?

**Opinion 1 (Shafi'i, Hanbali, Maliki in optional prayers)**: Say "Ameen" aloud when the imam recites Al-Fatihah aloud.

**Opinion 2 (Hanafi, Maliki in obligatory prayers)**: Say "Ameen" silently in all prayers.

**Evidence**: The Prophet ﷺ said, "When the imam says 'wa la ad-dallin,' say 'Ameen'" (Bukhari). The practice of the companions varied, with some narrations indicating audible "Ameen."

**Wisdom of Differences**: These differences reflect the broad mercy of Islamic law and the various authentic practices of the Prophet ﷺ in different contexts.

## Linguistic & Rhetorical Beauty

### 1. Structural Balance
The surah is perfectly balanced:
- 3.5 verses for Allah's attributes and praise
- 3.5 verses for the servant's requests
- Verse 5 serves as the pivot, connecting both halves

### 2. Progression of Divine Names
- **Allah**: The proper name, indicating the essence
- **Ar-Rahman**: General mercy in creation and provision
- **Ar-Rahim**: Specific mercy for believers in the afterlife
- **Rabb**: Lord, Master, Sustainer who nurtures creation
- **Malik**: Owner and Sovereign of the Day of Judgment

Each name builds upon the previous, creating a complete picture of our relationship with the Divine.

### 3. Pronoun Shift
- Verses 1-4: Third person (He - Allah)
- Verse 5 onward: Second person (You - Iyyaka)

This shift represents the progression from talking about Allah to talking to Allah—the essence of prayer.

### 4. Emphasis Through Word Order
In Arabic, placing the object before the verb adds emphasis. "Iyyaka na'budu" (You alone we worship) emphasizes exclusivity—not "we worship You," but "You alone we worship."

### 5. Inclusive Language
"We" instead of "I" indicates communal worship and humility, while also making the prayer applicable to every Muslim who recites it.

## Connection to Other Surahs

### With Surah Al-Baqarah
Al-Fatihah asks for guidance to the straight path; Al-Baqarah begins by identifying the Quran as "guidance for the God-conscious." The request is immediately answered.

### With Surah Al-Ikhlas
Al-Fatihah and Al-Ikhlas are called the two central pillars of the Quran—one focusing on Tawhid in worship (Al-Fatihah), the other on Tawhid in belief (Al-Ikhlas).

### With The Entire Quran
Al-Fatihah is called "Umm al-Kitab" (Mother of the Book) because it contains, in summary form, all the themes elaborated in the rest of the Quran:
- Theology (who Allah is)
- Eschatology (Day of Judgment)
- Human purpose (worship and seeking help)
- Guidance (the Quran as the straight path)
- Historical lessons (those who were favored, earned anger, or went astray)
