# References and Sources - Juz 1

This document lists the primary sources used for the tafsir of Juz 1 (Al-Fatihah and Al-Baqarah 1-141).

---

## Classical Tafsir Works

### 1. Tafsir Ibn Kathir (Tafsir al-Quran al-Azim)
**Author**: Ismail ibn Umar ibn Kathir (d. 774 AH / 1373 CE)  
**Methodology**: Tafsir al-Quran bil-Quran (Quran explains Quran), then authentic hadith, then statements of companions and successors  
**Strengths**: Rigorous hadith authentication, comprehensive coverage, accessible language  
**Used for**: Hadith connections, historical context, basic meanings

### 2. Al-Jami' li-Ahkam al-Quran (Tafsir Al-Qurtubi)
**Author**: Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Qurtubi (d. 671 AH / 1273 CE)  
**Methodology**: Emphasis on legal rulings (fiqh) derived from verses  
**Strengths**: Detailed exploration of jurisprudential implications, multiple scholarly opinions  
**Used for**: Legal applications, scholarly differences, practical implementations

### 3. Jami' al-Bayan fi Ta'wil al-Quran (Tafsir Al-Tabari)
**Author**: Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari (d. 310 AH / 923 CE)  
**Methodology**: Comprehensive compilation of early scholarly interpretations with linguistic analysis  
**Strengths**: Earliest comprehensive tafsir, preserves statements of Salaf, linguistic depth  
**Used for**: Early scholarly opinions, linguistic nuances, variant interpretations

### 4. Tafsir Al-Jalalayn
**Authors**: Jalal ad-Din al-Mahalli (d. 864 AH) and Jalal ad-Din as-Suyuti (d. 911 AH)  
**Methodology**: Concise, accessible commentary for students  
**Strengths**: Brief, clear, widely studied  
**Used for**: Quick reference, standard interpretations

### 5. Al-Kashshaf (Tafsir Al-Zamakhshari)
**Author**: Mahmud ibn Umar al-Zamakhshari (d. 538 AH / 1144 CE)  
**Methodology**: Linguistic and rhetorical analysis  
**Strengths**: Unparalleled linguistic insights, rhetorical beauty  
**Caution**: Author had Mu'tazilah theological leanings (rationalist school)  
**Used for**: Arabic linguistic features, rhetorical analysis (theology filtered through orthodox lens)

### 6. Anwar al-Tanzil wa Asrar al-Ta'wil (Tafsir Al-Baydawi)
**Author**: Nasir ad-Din al-Baydawi (d. 685 AH / 1286 CE)  
**Methodology**: Based on Al-Zamakhshari but with Sunni theology corrections  
**Used for**: Balanced linguistic analysis with orthodox theology

### 7. Al-Tafsir al-Kabir (Tafsir Al-Razi / Mafatih al-Ghayb)
**Author**: Fakhr ad-Din al-Razi (d. 606 AH / 1210 CE)  
**Methodology**: Philosophical and theological exploration  
**Strengths**: Deep rational arguments, addresses philosophical questions  
**Used for**: Theological depth, rational proofs, philosophical dimensions

### 8. Al-Tahrir wa al-Tanwir (Tafsir Ibn Ashur)
**Author**: Muhammad al-Tahir ibn Ashur (d. 1973 CE)  
**Methodology**: Comprehensive linguistic, legal, and thematic analysis  
**Strengths**: Modern scholarship with classical depth, Maqasid ash-Shariah emphasis  
**Used for**: Linguistic precision, objectives of Shariah, contemporary relevance

## Modern Tafsir Works

### 9. Fi Zilal al-Quran (In the Shade of the Quran)
**Author**: Sayyid Qutb (d. 1966 CE)  
**Methodology**: Thematic, literary, and socio-political interpretation  
**Strengths**: Powerful literary style, emphasis on Quranic worldview, practical application  
**Cautions**: Some controversial positions on politics and society  
**Used for**: Thematic unity, contemporary challenges, spiritual dimensions

### 10. Tafhim al-Quran (Toward Understanding the Quran)
**Author**: Abul A'la Maududi (d. 1979 CE)  
**Methodology**: Comprehensive modern commentary addressing contemporary issues  
**Strengths**: Accessible to modern readers, addresses current challenges  
**Used for**: Contemporary application, comparative religion perspectives

### 11. Tafsir al-Misbah (Tafsir of the Lamp)
**Author**: Muhammad Quraish Shihab (contemporary)  
**Methodology**: Classical scholarship applied to Indonesian/Southeast Asian context  
**Strengths**: Cultural relevance, modern scholarship, accessible  
**Used for**: Contemporary Muslim perspectives, practical applications

### 12. Al-Tafsir al-Munir (The Enlightening Commentary)
**Author**: Wahbah al-Zuhayli (d. 2015 CE)  
**Methodology**: Comprehensive modern tafsir covering linguistic, legal, and spiritual dimensions  
**Strengths**: Balanced, scholarly, comprehensive  
**Used for**: Legal rulings, balanced perspectives

### 13. The Study Quran
**Editors**: Seyyed Hossein Nasr et al. (2015)  
**Methodology**: Academic compilation drawing from multiple classical commentaries  
**Strengths**: Comprehensive scholarly notes, multiple viewpoints  
**Used for**: Academic reference, comparative classical views

## Hadith Collections

### Primary Collections (Kutub al-Sittah - The Six Books)

### 14. Sahih al-Bukhari
**Compiler**: Muhammad ibn Ismail al-Bukhari (d. 256 AH / 870 CE)  
**Status**: Most authentic hadith collection after the Quran  
**Contents**: ~7,563 hadith (with repetitions)  
**Used for**: Prophetic commentary on verses, historical context, practical application

### 15. Sahih Muslim
**Compiler**: Muslim ibn al-Hajjaj (d. 261 AH / 875 CE)  
**Status**: Second most authentic collection  
**Contents**: ~12,000 hadith (with repetitions)  
**Used for**: Alternative narrations, additional prophetic explanations

### 16. Sunan Abu Dawud
**Compiler**: Abu Dawud al-Sijistani (d. 275 AH / 889 CE)  
**Focus**: Legal rulings and practical guidance  
**Used for**: Jurisprudential applications

### 17. Jami' al-Tirmidhi (Sunan al-Tirmidhi)
**Compiler**: Muhammad ibn Isa al-Tirmidhi (d. 279 AH / 892 CE)  
**Feature**: Includes grading and scholarly opinions on each hadith  
**Used for**: Multiple scholarly views, authentication levels

### 18. Sunan al-Nasa'i
**Compiler**: Ahmad ibn Shu'ayb al-Nasa'i (d. 303 AH / 915 CE)  
**Focus**: High authentication standards  
**Used for**: Verified legal rulings

### 19. Sunan Ibn Majah
**Compiler**: Muhammad ibn Yazid ibn Majah (d. 273 AH / 887 CE)  
**Used for**: Additional narrations, supplementary evidence

### Other Important Hadith Collections

### 20. Musnad Ahmad
**Compiler**: Ahmad ibn Hanbal (d. 241 AH / 855 CE)  
**Contents**: ~27,000+ hadith organized by companion  
**Used for**: Comprehensive collection, additional narrations

### 21. Muwatta Malik
**Compiler**: Malik ibn Anas (d. 179 AH / 795 CE)  
**Status**: Earliest surviving hadith collection  
**Used for**: Early scholarly practice, Madani traditions

### 22. Hadith Authentication
**Contemporary Scholars Relied Upon**:
- Muhammad Nasir al-Din al-Albani (d. 1999) - Silsilat al-Ahadith as-Sahihah, Sahih al-Jami'
- Shu'ayb al-Arna'ut (d. 2016) - Authentication of Musnad Ahmad
- Abd al-Fattah Abu Ghuddah - Various authentication works

## Methodology of This Tafsir

### Source Hierarchy Used

**1. Quran Itself** (Tafsir al-Quran bil-Quran)
- Verses explain other verses
- Most authoritative interpretation

**2. Authentic Hadith**
- Prophetic explanations
- Limited to Sahih and Hassan graded narrations

**3. Statements of Companions** (Sahaba)
- Especially those who witnessed revelation
- Ibn Abbas, Ibn Mas'ud, etc.

**4. Statements of Successors** (Tabi'un)
- Students of companions
- Mujahid, Ikrimah, Qatadah, etc.

**5. Classical Scholarly Consensus** (Ijma)
- Where all or majority of scholars agree

**6. Analogical Reasoning** (Qiyas)
- Applying established principles to new situations

**7. Arabic Linguistics**
- Root meanings, classical usage
- Rhetorical features

**8. Historical Context**
- Asbab al-nuzul (where authentically reported)
- Makki/Madani classification

### Filters Applied

**Theological Filter**: 
- Orthodox Sunni theology (Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah)
- Filtering out deviant theological positions from otherwise valuable linguistic works

**Hadith Filter**:
- Only authentic (sahih) and good (hassan) hadith cited
- Weak narrations mentioned only if meaning is supported by other evidence
- Fabricated narrations rejected entirely

**Isra'iliyyat Filter**:
- Jewish/Christian sources treated with caution
- Three categories: corroborated, contradicted, neither
- Used only for historical context, never for creed or law

### Contemporary Application

**Principles**:
1. Understanding classical context is essential
2. Applying timeless principles to modern situations
3. Avoiding forced relevance or distorting meanings
4. Distinguishing between:
   - Universal principles (apply always)
   - Contextual applications (may vary)
