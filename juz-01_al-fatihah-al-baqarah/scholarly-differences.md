# Scholarly Differences (Ikhtilaf) - Juz 1

This document respectfully presents areas where classical and contemporary Islamic scholars have differed in their interpretation of verses in Juz 1. These differences reflect the richness of Islamic scholarship and demonstrate that within established boundaries, legitimate diversity of understanding exists.

**Principle**: "My ummah's disagreement is a mercy" - attributed to the Prophet ﷺ (authenticity debated, but principle widely accepted)

---

## Al-Fatihah

### 1. Is the Basmalah Part of Al-Fatihah?

#### Opinion A: Basmalah Is Verse 1 of Al-Fatihah
**Proponents**: Shafi'i school, one narration from Ahmad ibn Hanbal

**Evidence**:
1. The hadith qudsi dividing Al-Fatihah mentions it starts with "Alhamdu lillah" as if Basmalah precedes it as part of the surah
2. It is written in the mushaf at the beginning of Al-Fatihah
3. The Prophet ﷺ counted it among the seven verses
4. Reciting it is necessary for the completeness of Al-Fatihah in prayer

**Practical Implication**: Must be recited aloud when Al-Fatihah is recited aloud in prayer.

#### Opinion B: Basmalah Is a Separate Verse Between Surahs
**Proponents**: Hanafi, Maliki, and mainstream Hanbali schools

**Evidence**:
1. The Prophet ﷺ used to pause between the opening takbir and Al-Fatihah
2. Various narrations indicate the opening of Al-Fatihah is "Alhamdu lillahi Rabbi al-'alamin"
3. The Basmalah serves as a separator between surahs, revealed for that purpose
4. In narrations about the Prophet's prayer, companions describe hearing "Alhamdu lillah..." not Basmalah

**Practical Implication**: Should be recited silently before Al-Fatihah in prayer.

#### Reconciliation
**Ibn Taymiyyah's View**: Both practices are valid. The Prophet ﷺ sometimes recited it aloud and sometimes silently, giving flexibility to the ummah. The difference doesn't affect prayer validity.

**Contemporary Scholars**: This is a matter of fiqh (jurisprudence), not aqidah (creed). Muslims following any of these views are praying correctly according to authentic evidence from their school.

---

### 2. Reading "Maalik" vs "Malik" (Verse 4)

#### Qira'ah 1: مَالِكِ (Maaliki - King)
**Reciters**: Asim, Hamzah, Al-Kisa'i (among the seven canonical reciters)

**Meaning**: Sovereign, Ruler, King of the Day of Judgment

**Emphasis**: Dominion, authority, governance

#### Qira'ah 2: مَلِكِ (Maliki - Owner)
**Reciters**: Nafi', Ibn Kathir, Abu 'Amr (among the seven)

**Meaning**: Owner, Possessor of the Day of Judgment

**Emphasis**: Ownership, possession, control

#### Scholarly Consensus
Both readings are:
- Mutawatir (mass-transmitted, authentic)
- Valid in prayer
- Complementary, not contradictory
- Reflect different true attributes of Allah

**Ibn Kathir**: "Both readings are correct and mutawatir. Allah is both the King (Maalik) and Owner (Malik) of the Day of Judgment. One reading emphasizes sovereignty, the other ownership."

**Practical Implication**: A Muslim may recite either reading. Both are the Word of Allah.

### 3. Saying "Ameen" Aloud or Silently

#### Opinion A: Ameen Should Be Said Aloud
**Proponents**: Shafi'i, Hanbali, Maliki (in voluntary prayers)

**Evidence**:
1. The Prophet ﷺ said, "When the imam says 'wa la ad-dallin,' say 'Ameen'" - the apparent meaning is audible
2. The hadith mentions angels saying "Ameen" - audibly
3. Practice of many companions was to say it aloud
4. It's a du'a, not Quran, so rules about silent prayer don't apply

**Practical Implication**: Imam and congregation say "Ameen" audibly after Al-Fatihah in prayers recited aloud.

#### Opinion B: Ameen Should Be Said Silently
**Proponents**: Hanafi school, Maliki (in obligatory prayers)

**Evidence**:
1. General principle that recitation in silent prayers should be silent
2. Some narrations indicate the Prophet ﷺ would lower his voice at "Ameen"
3. Maintains the reverence and quiet of prayer
4. Saying it audibly was not consistently reported from all companions

**Practical Implication**: Everyone says "Ameen" silently in all prayers.

#### Reconciliation
Both practices have authentic basis. The Prophet ﷺ may have done both in different contexts. Contemporary scholars suggest:
- Following the practice of one's local community to maintain unity
- Respecting both views as legitimate
- Not criticizing those who follow the other opinion

## Al-Baqarah: Mysterious Letters

### 4. Meaning of "Alif Lam Mim" (Verse 1)

#### Opinion A: Only Allah Knows Their Meaning
**Proponents**: Majority of classical scholars including Abu Bakr as-Siddiq, Ibn Abbas (in one narration)

**Reasoning**:
- These are among the mutashabihat (unclear verses) mentioned in Surah Al-Imran 3:7
- No authentic explanation from the Prophet ﷺ has been preserved
- They test our submission—accepting what we don't fully understand
- Not everything in revelation is meant for human comprehension

**Ibn Kathir**: "The correct view is that only Allah knows what He intended by these letters."

#### Opinion B: They Have Meanings We Can Deduce
**Various Proposals**:

**1. Names of Allah**: Some scholars said they represent divine names
- Alif = Allah
- Lam = Latif (Subtle)
- Mim = Majid (Glorious)

**2. Oath Letters**: They are a form of divine oath to draw attention

**3. Challenge Letters**: "This Quran is composed of these letters you know and use, yet you cannot produce its like"

**4. Numerical Values**: Some scholars explored Arabic numerical meanings (abjad)

**5. Sounds to Grab Attention**: Like saying "Listen!" before important speech

**Counterargument**: None of these explanations have authentic chain to the Prophet ﷺ, making them speculative.

#### Balanced View (Al-Qurtubi)
"There are various opinions about these letters, but the most correct is that they are among the knowledge that Allah has kept to Himself. However, we can say with certainty that they serve to:
1. Draw attention to what follows
2. Emphasize the miraculous nature of the Quran
3. Test our submission to accept what Allah has revealed even without full comprehension"

## Story of Adam

### 5. Did Angels Object to Adam's Creation? (Verse 30)

#### Opinion A: It Was Inquiry, Not Objection
**Proponents**: Majority of scholars including Ibn Abbas, Al-Qurtubi

**Reasoning**:
- Angels are incapable of disobedience (Quran 66:6)
- Their question was seeking knowledge, not challenging Allah's wisdom
- They asked based on their limited understanding of previous earthly creatures
- The question form in Arabic (istifham) can mean inquiry

**Al-Qurtubi**: "The angels did not object. They asked to learn and understand Allah's wisdom, which He then explained to them."

#### Opinion B: It Was Humble Objection Corrected by Allah
**Proponents**: Some scholars including aspects mentioned by Ibn Kathir

**Reasoning**:
- The question could imply concern or mild objection
- Allah corrected their understanding by showing them what they didn't know
- This doesn't mean disobedience—they immediately accepted the correction
- Shows that even angels have limited knowledge

**Ibn Kathir**: "Whether it was inquiry or humble expression of concern, the angels immediately submitted once Allah showed them His wisdom."

#### Reconciliation
Both views maintain:
- Angels' perfect obedience and submission
- Their inherent limitations in knowledge
- Allah's perfect wisdom in creation
- The story's purpose: teaching humans about their special role

### 6. What Was the Forbidden Tree? (Verse 35)

#### Various Opinions:
**A. Wheat tree** (Ibn Abbas, in one narration)  
**B. Grape vine** (Some Salaf)  
**C. Fig tree** (Others among early scholars)  
**D. A tree unique to Paradise, no earthly equivalent** (Some scholars)

#### Opinion E: Allah Did Not Specify, So Neither Should We
**Proponents**: Ibn Kathir, many contemporary scholars

**Reasoning**:
1. No authentic hadith specifies the tree
2. The lesson is in obedience, not botanical identification
3. If knowing the tree type was important, Allah would have clearly stated it
4. Speculation on unspecified matters is discouraged

**Ibn Kathir**: "What is important is that it was a real tree, and disobeying Allah's clear command—regardless of the specific tree—resulted in consequences."

**Practical Implication**: Focus on the moral lesson (obedience, repentance) rather than details Allah chose not to reveal.

---

### 7. Where Was Paradise—Earthly or Heavenly?

#### Opinion A: The Eternal Paradise (Jannah)
**Proponents**: Majority including Ibn Abbas, Hasan al-Basri

**Reasoning**:
- The word used is "Jannah" (Paradise)
- Allah commanded angels to prostrate, and they were in Paradise
- Iblis was there, and he had access to Paradise before his rebellion
- They were expelled from Paradise to earth

#### Opinion B: An Earthly Garden
**Proponents**: Some Mu'tazilah and rationalist scholars

**Reasoning**:
- How can sin occur in eternal Paradise?
- Paradise is a place of no sadness or expulsion
- It may have been a special earthly garden

**Majority Response**:
- It was the eternal Paradise, but Adam and Hawwa were not yet permanent residents—they were being tested
- After successful completion of test, they would have stayed eternally
- The test failing led to temporary earthly life before eventual return
- No contradiction: Paradise has different stages and conditions

**Ibn Taymiyyah**: "It was the Paradise from which believers will never be expelled, but Adam was there conditionally during his test period."

## Bani Israel

### 8. The Golden Calf—How Did It Moo? (Verse 51)

**Context**: Some verses mention the calf made a mooing sound.

#### Opinion A: Samiri Created a Hollow Structure That Made Sound
**Proponents**: Rationalist scholars

**Explanation**: The calf was crafted so that when wind passed through it, it produced a sound resembling mooing—a clever deception, not a miracle.

#### Opinion B: Allah Allowed It to Make Sound as a Test
**Proponents**: Majority of classical scholars

**Explanation**: Allah allowed this as a fitnah (trial). Samiri had learned sorcery or was assisted by Shaytan. The sound was supernatural, making the test harder.

**Ibn Kathir**: "Whether natural (clever crafting) or supernatural (Allah's test), what matters is that Bani Israel failed by worshiping a created object despite knowing it didn't create itself."

#### Practical Lesson
Focus: Not the mechanism of deception, but the foolishness of worshiping creation despite clear evidence of its created nature.

---

### 9. Extent of Bani Israel Narratives' Application

#### Opinion A: Primarily Historical
**View**: These verses address the Jewish community in Madinah specifically, documenting historical failures.

**Limited Application**: Muslims can draw general lessons, but the verses aren't primarily about Muslims.

#### Opinion B: Universal Warning for Muslims
**View**: The extensive treatment of Bani Israel serves as direct warning to Muslims about patterns to avoid.

**Proponents**: Sayyid Qutb, contemporary scholars

**Evidence**: 
- The Prophet's ﷺ hadith: "You will follow the ways of those before you..."
- The Quran uses past nations as lessons for present and future
- The detailed treatment suggests more than mere historical record

#### Balanced Synthesis (Recommended)
**Both dimensions are true**:
1. **Historical**: Real events addressing real contemporary audience (Jews of Madinah)
2. **Universal**: Lessons and warnings applicable to all, especially Muslims who now carry the covenant

**Application**: Muslims should:
- Study these passages as direct warnings
- Identify parallel behaviors in Muslim communities
- Actively guard against repetition of these errors

## The Qiblah

### 10. Qiblah Direction Before the Change (Verse 142-144)

#### Opinion A: Always Toward the Ka'bah
**Proponents**: Some scholars

**Evidence**: The Ka'bah was established by Ibrahim, and Muslims naturally oriented toward it from the beginning.

#### Opinion B: Toward Jerusalem in Madinah, Ka'bah in Makkah
**Proponents**: Majority

**Evidence**: 
- Al-Bara' ibn Azib's authentic hadith: "We prayed toward Jerusalem for sixteen or seventeen months"
- The Prophet ﷺ desired to face the Ka'bah, indicating he wasn't already doing so
- The verse about the change only makes sense if there was an actual change

#### Opinion C: Positioned to Face Both in Makkah
**Some narrations suggest**: In Makkah, the Prophet ﷺ positioned himself so that Jerusalem was in front and the Ka'bah behind, thus praying toward both.

**Most Authenticated View (Ibn Kathir)**: 
- In Makkah: Toward the Ka'bah
- After Hijrah in Madinah: Toward Jerusalem for 16-17 months
- Then: Command came to turn toward the Ka'bah

**Wisdom of Change**:
1. Test of obedience—true believers follow the Messenger
2. Establish distinct Islamic identity
3. Fulfill the Prophet's ﷺ desire
4. Symbolize the reclaiming of Abrahamic legacy

---

### 11. Must One Face Exact Direction or General Direction?

#### Opinion A: Exact Precision Required
**Proponents**: Some literalists

**Reasoning**: The verse says "turn your face toward" the Masjid al-Haram.

#### Opinion B: General Direction Sufficient
**Proponents**: Majority of scholars across all schools

**Evidence**:
- The Prophet ﷺ said: "What is between the east and the west is qiblah" (for those praying from Madinah)
- Companions prayed in various locations without sophisticated direction tools
- Allah says "Wherever you turn, there is the face of Allah" (2:115)
- The requirement is reasonable effort to determine direction

**Practical Implication**: Use available means (sun, stars, compass, apps), make sincere effort, then trust prayer is valid.

## Methodological Issues

### 12. Can We Use Isra'iliyyat (Narratives from Jewish Sources)?

**Context**: Many tafsir books include stories about prophets from Jewish sources not in Quran or authentic hadith.

#### Opinion A: Permissible with Conditions
**Proponents**: Ibn Kathir, majority of classical scholars

**Conditions**:
1. Don't affirm or deny if not corroborated by Islamic sources
2. Mention them as possibilities, not certainties
3. Useful for historical context, not creed or law
4. Must not contradict Quran or authentic hadith

**Hadith Basis**: "Narrate from Bani Israel, and there is no harm. They have amazing stories" (Bukhari).

#### Opinion B: Should Be Avoided
**Proponents**: Some contemporary scholars

**Reasoning**:
- Risk of corrupted information
- Can confuse lay Muslims
- Allah revealed what's necessary; additions are unnecessary
- Modern Muslims lack the scholarly discretion to filter properly

#### Middle Way (Recommended for Students)
- Recognize that classical tafsir includes Isra'iliyyat
- Understand the three categories: corroborated, contradicted, neither
- Focus study on Quran and authentic hadith
- Treat Isra'iliyyat as historical curiosity, not religious knowledge

## Principles for Dealing with Ikhtilaf

### 1. Distinguish Types of Differences

**A. Tanawwu' (Diversity)**
- Different but complementary views
- All correct, just emphasizing different aspects
- Example: Different meanings of "guidance" in verse 6

**B. Tadad (Genuine Disagreement)**
- Mutually exclusive positions
- Evidence supports multiple views
- Example: Aloud vs silent Ameen

### 2. Hierarchy of Issues

**Usul (Fundamentals)**: No tolerance for difference
- Tawhid, prophethood, afterlife
- Unity required

**Furu' (Branches)**: Legitimate differences allowed
- Jurisprudence, some interpretations
- Diversity is mercy

### 3. Etiquette of Disagreement

**Requirements**:
- Respect for all views within orthodoxy
- Acknowledge evidence for different positions
- Avoid takfir (declaring others disbelievers) over jurisprudential matters
- Unite on fundamentals while respecting differences in details

**Ibn Taymiyyah**: "Issues of ijtihaad (scholarly reasoning) where multiple views exist—one doesn't condemn those following valid differences."

### 4. For the Layperson

**Guidance**:
1. Follow qualified scholars of your school
2. Don't mock other legitimate schools
3. Focus on action, not arguing over details
4. When in doubt, follow local majority for community unity
5. Respect that diversity in furu' doesn't weaken Islam—it shows richness

## Conclusion

The differences mentioned in this document:
- Reflect deep scholarship and engagement with texts
- Show that Islam has space for legitimate diversity
- Do not undermine Islamic unity on fundamentals
- Teach humility—none of us has monopoly on truth
- Remind us that Allah's knowledge exceeds all

**From the Prophet ﷺ**: "My companions are like stars; whichever of them you follow, you will be guided" (Authenticated versions suggest support for following any legitimate scholarly opinion).

**Final Word**: These differences exist within the framework of established Islamic orthodoxy. They are not contradictions but different facets of comprehensive divine guidance.
